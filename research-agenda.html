Research Agenda

My research puts forth a methodology that examines performances of gender and sexuality in objects such as computer code and network protocols. Scholars across fields like queer activism, feminist technology studies, digital rhetoric, and technical communication have begun to examine the social implications of code. While these scholars have explored code as a form of writing, as the construction of rhetorical space, or as an argumentative text, my scholarship specifically examines code as gender performativity. Following this, I analyze  how programming techniques and practices--evident in source code--articulate heteronormative structures of labor and community in technical fields like computer programming. I use formal methods of reading code drawn from digital rhetoric and critical code studies, but inform them with theories of queer performativity and new media studies to establish a methodology for research that approaches different software as instances of gender performativity that connect the practices of professionals in the field, technical communicators, and every day end users. I conclude that there are concrete connections between how software is developed, the performative nature of that software, and how gendered social formations and discourses emerge from that software, and that a methodology of studying these connections provides an approach to recognize and enact possibilities activism and subversion at the level of software engineering.

 

In the development of my approach to code, I am inspired by the activist work of queer artists and activist such as Zach Blas and Sandy Stone as well as theorists of gender performance and sexuality such as Judith Butler, J. Jack Halberstam, Jose Esteban Munoz, and Eve Kosofsky Sedgewick. Theories of performativity, periperformativity, and the power of performance to subvert structural norms informs my training in rhetoric and writing and my background in computer science. I have explored some of the implications of the intersections of these influence through several recent and upcoming publications, resulting with a methodology of approaching code that reads code and code logic as practices of rhetorical articulation that perform gender as a communicative epistemology. My dissertation, Queer Practices, Queer Rhetoric, Queer Technology: Studies of Digital Performativity in Gendered Network Culture, traces queer digital artwork, production-level code such as BitTorrent and Bitcoin, and services such as the Egyptian activist site Hrassmap to identify the perforative and productive liinks between code, protocols, and emerging formations of gendered collaboration and labor.

 

I have established a research agenda based on this methodology, and have developed aspects of this scholarship through publication in JOGLTEP: Journal of Global Literacies, Technologies, and Emerging Pedagogies and the edited collection Rhetoric, Writing, and Circulation. I also have an article under consideration with QED: A Journal of GLBTQ Worldmaking that outlines the foundations for reading code as a performance of gender and as a mode of potential activism. Future research will trace the implications of my research across the open-source Linux development community, and explore the connections of gender, code, and programming languages in the historical and contemporary development of artificial intelligence.
